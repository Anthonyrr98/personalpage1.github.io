The Clearing

I was moved by The Clearing from JJJJerome Ellis, an “other-abled” musician unafraid to investigate his stutter on record. More essay than album, it includes periods of total silence, or “clearings”, as Ellis describes the gaps in his speech. It’s also a powerfully poetic reflection on Blackness and resistance.

A vision for Broad Marsh

Here’s Thomas Heatherwick’s vision for Broad Marsh, the ruins of which are an embarrassing, lingering blight on Nottingham. It’d be hard to deliver on these renders, but he makes all the right points, and I’d love to see us aim this high. Chief obstacles will be a reduction of ambition by committee and increasingly noisy local defeatists.

Spotify Wrapped & Apple Replay

I do like the idea of Wrapped, but gets better as a marketing ploy and worse as an experience. Last year, I wrote that it falls short of any real insight. This year, it suffered from laborious pacing, increased memeification and try-hard copy appealing to a music-lite demographic.

Apple Music gets a bashing on Wrapped day, but it does offer an expanded Replay with hours listened, top artists and albums. It’s refreshingly free of bullshit but inexplicably hidden away in the browser.

Anyway, here are my stats (excluding Bandcamp and physical media). I listened for 68,366 minutes (Spotify 32,786, Apple 35,580; I switched in July).

Spotify top artists: Ólafur Arnalds, The Antlers, Manics, Bicep, Floating Points. Spotify top songs: Lucid Express, Wellwave; Ólafur Arnalds, Loom; Bicep, Atlas; Ólafur Arnalds, Spiral and Woven Song.

Apple top artists: Radiohead, The War on Drugs, Deafheaven, Squirrel Flower, Japanese Breakfast. Apple top songs: Squirrel Flower, I’ll Go Running; Radiohead, Reckoner; Lucid Express, Hotel 65; Deafheaven, Great Mass of Colour; Lucid Express, Wellwave.

Finally, here are the albums I’ve listened to the most since the switch, a 7/10 match to my current Year in Music draft: Squirrel Flower, Planet (1); Japanese Breakfast, Jubilee; Deafheaven, Infinite Granite; Space Afrika, Honest Labour; Lucid Express, Lucid Express; Nala Sinephro, Space 1.8; Radiohead, In Rainbows; Flaoting Points, Promises; Low, Hey What; Big Red Machine, How Long....

1st December 2021 7:30pm

Songs about cities and buildings

I have a weird thing for songs about cities and buildings. Talking Heads, The Cure, Simple Minds, The Manics, Drahla and many more have offered strange images of cold urban spaces and formulaic patterns for living.

The title track of coldwave duo The KVB’s new album does exactly this. Informed by Le Corbusier’s Unité d’habitation, the lyrics describe a dystopian cityscape of concrete and steel: Reconstruction of a nation; Future concrete occupation; Linear industrial city; Modular factory living. I have no time for Le Corbusier’s grand schemes (authoritarian) or his political views (fascist) but I like this song.

Radiohead, again. But, you know, best band in the world, our generation’s Beatles, etc. Anyway, last night I got lost in the disconcertingly claustrophobic KID A MNESIA exhibition, and somehow I didn’t have nightmares.

Notes on Web3

Just as we transitioned swiftly from Web Zero to Web 2.0, we will now accelerate toward “Web3”, a new ownership-driven, crypto-centric epoch. It’s mostly about money, and we’re doomed.

Although Robin Sloan’s NFT dabbling makes him complicit, his notes on Web3 are reasonably balanced and can aid our critical thinking. For example, he offers us old curmudgeons an appropriate perspective, considering how this future excites those young enough to crave something genuinely new and entirely theirs.

Update: Robin Rendle’s Who is web3 for? is a valuable extension of Sloan’s notes.

11th November 2021 3:20pm

Wild About Kinder

Back in 1998, during my artist residency at Losehill Hall, someone arranged for two Polish biologists and me to spend an unforgettable day on Kinderscout with Peak District National Park ranger Gordon Miller.

To kick off her new podcast, Wild About Kinder, host Sarah Lister interviews Gordon, now 80, about his time on the mountain and his path from fascinated young hiker to respected ranger. Gordon recounts a lifetime of observations, including the undeniable signs of pollution, significant incidents on the plateau, and the importance of sharing knowledge.

There is so much to value, but I especially loved his reply when asked what was in his heart that made him want to become a ranger: “Kinder. Kinder dominated everything. Kinder had always been the iconic place. Always.”

9th November 2021 12:40pm

Macdonald and Dean on Lanyon

A few months back, I noted a passage by John Berger that pinpoints what I most love about Peter Lanyon’s work. To follow, here’s Helen Macdonald on Thermal, a painting I spent some time with on our recent trip to Cornwall:

Thermal is a portrait of the dynamic complexities of air. There’s no singular vantage on the scene, though it’s not hard to see that the upwards surge of pale paint from its base must be a column of rising air, the fat square of complicated greys a turbulent region near its top, and the strokes on the right a region of cooling, sinking aerial decay. To me, Thermal is a painting that evokes an experience, rather than merely showing me what it looks like. It makes palpable the sensation of being in a glider. It pulls my body into the experience it depicts. I can almost feel the crackling in my inner ear as I rise, the sense deep in my bones of being tilted and pushed around by tonnes of rising and falling air. I love Lanyon’s painting most of all for this: however fiercely we are taught that the natural world exists out there, Thermal is a reminder that we’re in the environment, all of us; we’re not just walking around on it or looking at it from afar. It’s all over us, it is in us, it is through us. It is us.
— Helen Macdonald, Reading The Skies, Tate, 2020

I also found a note by Tacita Dean on the shift in Lanyon’s work after he took up gliding: “His paintings begin to lose the anxiety and something of the heaviness of earth and the old land.” It was in the sky, she says, that Lanyon found his “elsewhere”.

5th November 2021 12:20pm

Kid A Mnesia

With the release of Kid A Mnesia, there’s been a welcome dump of material looking back at Radiohead’s make-or-break period. If, like me, you cannot separate the music from its intrinsic visual language, check out this short studio interview with Thom Yorke and Stanley Donwood, this in-depth discussion, and the forthcoming book of artwork. For broader context, nod along with today’s excellently chin-rubby Pitchfork and The Quietus deep-dives.

I’m obsessed with the intertwingling of art, sound and ideas about landscape, so when Thom talks about these things, I’m there. And I love his point about a naive process, reminiscent of McLuhan’s praise for the amateur.

The nature of being a songwriter, or a painter, or whatever, is to retain a beginner’s mind. The search is the point. The flailing around is the point. The process is the point.
— Thom Yorke, Kid A Amnesia: A Book of Radiohead Artwork

Anyway, this post serves as another reminder to move closer to the things you love because they will help you expand. And with that, I’m off to pick up my copy of the album.

1st November 2021 6:30pm

Ableton turns 20

Ableton Live, the much-loved music production and performance workstation, just turned 20. I’ve been using Ableton for only one year, but it became my favourite software almost overnight. Everything about its approach, especially the unique dual session and arrangement views, made sense to me right away, and no matter how deep I go, there’s rarely any friction. I seem to have a nice time whenever I fire it up.

What’s remarkable is how familiar that first version looks. As Elliot notes, “Ableton Live’s interface has barely changed in 20 years because it has always been just so, so good. Not many apps out there that can iterate this gently over two decades.”

Jazzing things up

I’ve listened to more jazz than ever this year, which wasn’t hard because rarely did I ever listen to jazz. Thanks to Geri’s growing interest in past masters, I’ve finally made sense of Davis, Coltrane and Evans, and enjoyed less obvious Japanese cats (couldn’t resist, sorry) like Kohsuke Mine and Ryo Fukui. Most thrilling has been all the brand new stuff I’ve discovered, some of which strays into classical, hip-hop and/or electronic territory.

Faves released this year include Sons of Kemet’s Black To The Future, Ishmael Ensemble’s Visions of Light, Alfa Mist’s Bring Backs and Portico Quartet’s Terrain. Several works will feature in my top ten of 2021: Floating Points and Pharaoh Sanders’ Promises (easily number one, written about twice previously), Nala Sinephro’s transcendent Space 1.8, and Jaubi’s Hindustani classical-inspired Nafs at Peace.

Another, BadBadNotGood’s Talk Memory, has just arrived, and it’s terrific, revealing more depth with every listen. I particularly love Unfolding (Momentum 73) with its interplay between Laraaji’s bubbling zither and Leland Whitty’s breathy sax (the latter as pleasing to my ear as Sanders’ improvised textures on Promises). The album is beautifully presented and accompanied by a set of Memory Catalogue poster zines. Plenty in which to lose myself.

Plus Equals #3

Today brings a new issue of Plus Equals, Rob Weychert’s algorithmic art zine. For this one, he created a set of 425 tiles, any of which can seamlessly border any other on any side, with a pleasingly bonkers payoff.

Rob’s unabashed enjoyment at compiling these zines is one of the things that’s encouraged me to revisit my old journals and start making shit again. His sharp focus reminds me that it’s ok to be curious, dive in real deep, and make things because you have the compulsion to make them and need them to exist. If other people find value in those things, that’s a bonus.

29th September 2021 6:20pm

Vibes

Nameless Feeling is a typically heady Real Life essay, in which Ludwig Yeetgenstein considers vibes. In particular, he examines the ways neural networks influence the patterns we encounter many times a day.

I particularly appreciate the way he shames the algorithm’s inability to apply logic to culture with all its ambiguities. It is a failure that underpins the lack of imagination — the stale loop — we all recognise.

Yeetgenstein doubts a future where beauty is measurable by one-dimensional metrics. We can measure beauty to a point, but a new image of beauty is constantly forming. I often think back to John Berger describing beauty as “an exception”, and I like that because exceptions are the enemy of algorithms.
